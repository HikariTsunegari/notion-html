SUMMARY

To systematically characterize the loss of tissue integrity and organ dysfunction resulting from aging, we pro-
duced an in-depth spatial transcriptomic profile of nine tissues in male mice during aging. We showed that
senescence-sensitive spots (SSSs) colocalized with elevated entropy in organizational structure and that
the aggregation of immunoglobulin-expressing cells is a characteristic feature of the microenvironment sur-
rounding SSSs. Immunoglobulin G (IgG) accumulated across the aged tissues in both male and female mice,
and a similar phenomenon was observed in human tissues, suggesting the potential of the abnormal eleva-
tion of immunoglobulins as an evolutionarily conserved feature in aging. Furthermore, we observed that IgG
could induce a pro-senescent state in macrophages and microglia, thereby exacerbating tissue aging, and
that targeted reduction of IgG mitigated aging across various tissues in male mice. This study provides a
high-resolution spatial depiction of aging and indicates the pivotal role of immunoglobulin-associated senes-
cence during the aging process.

Accumulation of senescent cells is causally linked to func-
tional decline in aging tissues.24–28 Senescent cells release
pro-inflammatory molecules, such as cytokines and chemo-
kines, collectively known as the senescence-associated secre-
tory phenotype (SASP), that can harm neighboring non-senes-
cent cells and contribute to age-related diseases.4,29–31
However, senescent cells are heterogeneous in their geograph-
ical distribution and molecular characteristics, both within and
across organs.32,33 Consequently, analyzing the localization
and environment of senescent cells and their interactions with
neighboring cells holds significant potential for advancing our
understanding of aging drivers and may also help identify poten-
tial intervention targets.

Here, we delineated the Gerontological Geography (GG) for
male mice by profiling the expansive spatiotemporal transcrip-
tomic landscape of tissue aging across consecutive life stages.
Furthermore, we have spatially characterized senescence-sen-
sitive spots (SSSs) and their microenvironments. Through these
efforts, we discovered that immunoglobulin-expressing cells
localize in the vicinity of the SSS-centered niche, facilitating tis-
sue aging. Additionally, we confirmed the consistent accumula-
tion of immunoglobulins across species and genders during ag-
ing, evident in both mice and humans. Crucially, immunoglobulin
G (IgG) aggregation emerges as a pivotal catalyst in tissue
senescence, presenting a promising molecular target for aging
interventions.

Spatially resolved age-related gene expression
alterations in male mice tissues
Next, to delineate cell-type-dependent aging-related alterations
at spatial resolution, we conducted an in-depth analysis of differ-
entially expressed genes (DEGs) between young and aged tis-
sues and across different spatial locations (Figures S4C and
S4D; Table S2). This comprehensive approach highlighted a
key finding: a substantial proportion of aging-related DEGs ex-
hibited both spatial and cell-type-specific expression patterns
(Figure S4D). For instance, aging-induced notable changes in
specific spatial regions, including the neural progenitor cell
area in the hippocampal DG, the middle region of gray matter
(GM) in the spinal cord, ventricular cardiomyocytes in the heart,
and zone 1 hepatocytes in liver. In lymphoid tissues, such as the
spleen and lymph nodes, pronounced changeswere observed in
T and B cells within the white pulp and B cells in the follicles,
respectively (Figures S4C and S4D). Of note, aging-related
DEGs also displayed spatial heterogeneity within the similar
cell types. For example, in the heart, numbers of such DEGs
were higher in ventricular cardiomyocytes than in those in the
atrial region, whereas in the liver, we noted a higher number of
DEGs in zone 1 hepatocytes than in zone 2 and 3 hepatocytes
(Figures S4C and S4D).

mation, mitochondrial dysfunction, and disrupted nutrient
sensing (Figures S4E and S5A–S5F). Upregulated DEGs were
mainly associated with inflammation in lymph nodes, spleens,
small intestine, and lungs, particularly in plasmocytes of aged
lymph node medulla and spleen red pulp (Figures S4E and
S5A). In comparison, downregulated DEGs correlated with mito-
chondrial function in the lymph node, hippocampus, and small
intestine (Figures S4E and S5B). For example, we observed

lated apoptosis-related genes in neural progenitor cells and
mature neural cells within the hippocampus DG and CA1-3 re-
gions (Figures 4E and S5C). Moreover, the weakened expression
of genes was associated with mitochondrial function pathways
and fatty acid metabolism in hepatocytes of the hepatic lobule
region within the liver, highlighting how spatially specific
dysfunction in energy dynamics and metabolic function impacts

Spatial mapping of SSSs and their niches in male mouse
tissues
Next, we shifted focus to pinpoint regions harboring senescence
hotspots and characterize their adjacent microenvironments. To
achieve this goal, we leveraged five well-established senes-
cence gene sets extensively utilized in prior studies intersected
with spot type-specific DEGs from each tissue.39–42 Based on
our aging DEGs and aging-related gene set, we computed an ag-
ing score for each spatial spot across various tissues (Figure 3A;
Table S2, see STAR Methods). By ranking spots based on their
aging scores, our approach enables the identification of regions
in different tissues with heightened susceptibility to aging. We
designated the top 5% of spots with the highest aging scores
in each aged sample as SSSs (Figure 3B). We noted that the in-
tensity of aging scores peaked at the center of the SSS and grad-
ually decreased outward to peripheral cells in a distance-depen-
dent manner (Figure 3C). Intriguingly, our analysis revealed that
areas with increased OSE scores and decreased cell identity
scores tended to cluster near the SSS (Figures 3D–3F and S6A).

We then dissected the molecular gradient characteristics sur-
rounding the SSS by ranking gene expression based on the
proximity to the SSS (Figures 3G, 3H, and S6B; Table S2). In
this way, we found within the SSS, humoral immune response
and complement activation were elevated, and proximity to the
SSS correlated with higher SASP scores (Figures 3G, 3H, and
S6C). Consistently, TNF-signaling gradually decreases with
increasing distance from the SSS (Figure S6D). By contrast,
the expression levels of genes associated with ATP biosynthetic
process and cell cycle were restored with increasing distance
from the SSS (Figures 3G and 3H). We consistently observed
this spatial pattern across multiple organs in aged mice
(Figures S6A–S6D). These findings suggest that SSSs serve as
epicenters for heightened inflammation that compromises sur-
rounding cells in a distance-dependent manner.

Next, we investigated the cellular identity of SSSs at a spatial
resolution. In the hippocampus, we detected oligodendrocyte
(OL) and CA2&CA3 neurons as the most prevalent SSSs
(Figures 3I and S6E–S6H). In the aged hippocampus, the SSS
spatial pattern coincided with the distribution of SPiDER-bGal-
positive cells, which were preferentially located within the
neuronal regions (Figure S6I). In the spinal cord, almost half of
SSSs were neuronal spots in the middle-GM region, whereas
in the heart, myocardial cells were predominant (Figures 3I and
S6G). Similarly, alveolar epithelial cells in the lung, zone 2 hepa-
tocytes in the liver, and the androgen-producing Leydig cells in
the testis were the main SSS components (Figures 3I and S6J).

symbiotic system

and their microenvironments, we analyzed the most frequently
occurring DEGs and examined their spatial relationships with
SSSs. Across tissues, the most highly upregulated genes were
those encoding immunoglobulins, such as Igkc, Jchain (Igj),
Ighg1, and Igha, and those encoding complement proteins,
such as C4b and C1qa (Figures 4A and S7F). Of those, Igkc,

lymph node, and small intestine (Figure S7G). In general, the pro-

(Figures 4C and 4D). By western blot analysis, we confirmed
the extraordinary accumulation of light and heavy IgG chains,
as well as Igkc, across various tissues (Figures S8A and S8B).

(Figures 5D and 5E). Thus, we have identified a consistent
pattern of intra-tissue IgG accumulation across multiple life
stages, sexes, and species, supporting that our observations
represent a widespread phenomenon in mammals and could
serve as a marker of human aging.

DISCUSSION

Maintenance of organizational spatial compartmentalization is a
hallmark of organismal health, while aging is associated with a
decline in this capacity.56,57 However, our understanding of how
spatial compartmentalization and structural organization are
compromised in aging is incomplete. Here, we introduce GG, the
first high-precision aging spatial transcriptomic atlas that spans
multiple organs and lifetime stages in male mice. Leveraging GG,
we document increased structural entropy in aged tissues, re-
flected as reduced polarity and regionalization and loss of cell
identity. We also introduce SSSs as a conceptual framework to
investigate senescent cells and their interactions with the macro-
and microenvironment. Our characterization of SSS cellular and
molecular signatures revealed that immunoglobulin-producing
cells in various tissues preferentially localize near SSSs, thereby

Spatial transcriptomics methods enable the creation of
detailed molecular maps with location information that is lost in
scRNAseq.19,20,58 Previously, this technology has been utilized

mouse brain, regenerating axolotl telencephalons, aging ovary,

structural entropy across nine distinct tissues and organs from

tissues such as the spleen and lymph nodes. These aging-asso-

ished cell identity, andalterations in cell proportions. Notably, ag-

with and extending from previous studies supporting that inflam-
mation is a prominent feature of systemic aging.4,11,13,17,47,62–64

The accumulation of senescent cells contributes to tissue and
organismal aging and is a well-known aging hallmark.56,65–71 Es-
tablished methodologies frequently rely on the identification of
senescent cells through specific biomarkers, such as SA-b-Gal
staining.72 However, these markers alone cannot comprehen-
sively characterize senescent cells and their surrounding envi-
ronment. To delve deeper into the drivers of senescence, we
introduce the concept of the SSS, emphasizing its context-,
location-, and tissue-specific nature. Notably, our research has
revealed a large amount of immunoglobulin-producing cells in
the vicinity of SSSs, which constitute an essential part of the
SSS microenvironment. Furthermore, our findings indicate that
heightened IgG, a key humoral immune factor, may induce
cellular senescence, enhance chronic inflammation, and accel-
erate tissue aging, corroborated by evidence that IgG can pro-
mote fibrosis in adipose tissue.55

Limitations of the study
Confronted with the logistical hurdles of analyzing samples from
a range of tissues and five stages, our study prioritized male
mice. Future efforts should target a thorough spatial transcrip-
tomic analysis in female mammals. With our rich dataset, we
foresee the discovery of more aging-related targets and path-
ways. Yet, constrained by article length, we highlighted immuno-
globulin-induced senescence. Further investigation should
explore the mechanisms of IgG in cellular aging and develop
strategies to reduce its levels or counteract its effects, possibly
leading to the development of immunoglobulin-targeted thera-
pies to combat age-related health decline.

Tissue collection
After anesthesia and dissection, tumor-bearing individuals, specifically the 25-month-old and the 19-month-old male were initially
excluded. Subsequently, a range of tissues (heart, lung, liver, small intestine, testis, spleen, mesenteric lymph node, spinal cord,
and hippocampus) from randomly selected mice across both young and old cohorts were collected. Subsequently, the tissues
were extracted and immediately rinsed with PBS solution containing 0.4 U/mL SUPERase In RNase inhibitor (Invitrogen,
AM2694). Excess liquid was gently blotted from the tissue surface using absorbent paper. Tissue samples from the same regional
location were then directly immersed in OCT compound (Sakura, 4583) and placed in Cryo-molds, with pre-chilled OCT at the bot-
tom, and kept on dry ice. Throughout the entire procedure, utmost care was taken to maintain RNase-free conditions. Additionally,
consistent placement and polarity of samples were ensured within each tissue. Once the samples were completely frozen on dry ice,

Spatially connected domain identification analysis
To identify the connectivity degree of MZmacro spot, we identified connected domains of MZmacro spot in eight-connected neigh-
borhoods by drawing on digital image processing techniques. We used the Seed-Filling algorithm, as follows:

1. Filter MZ macro spot for each sample.
2. Search for a start spot (seed) of a new region.
3. Search all spots in the slice that are connected with the seed spot and mark them to indicate their association.
4. Repeat steps 2 and 3 until all spots are marked.

Using this algorithm, we could identify the connectivity of MZmacro spot and calculate the connectivity degree of each connected
area (the number of spots in the connected area). We set 5 as the threshold for the connectivity degree. If greater than the threshold,
then it is adjacent; if less than the threshold, then it is non-adjacent.
